Women in Sport

Athletes

There have been many improvements to gender equality in sport over the years. Since 2012 women have competed in an equal number of sports as men at the Olympic games. Women’s exposure in sports has generally been unequal, making up just 7% of all sports media coverage in the UK. The interest in watching women’s sport is on the rise. Tennis, swimming, and athletics are all becoming equally popular to watch for both men and women. Other sports like women’s football, rugby and cricket people are less interested in watching. This is likely to change in the near future.

With such role models as Steph Houghton, Nicola Adams, Serena Williams, and many more its no wonder the next generation of women in sport is expected to bring more equality and empowerment both in sporting events and the media.

Broadcasters

Many women from sporting backgrounds also pursue careers related to sport. Some become sports management consultants, some become presenters etc. Old Arsenal defender Alex Scott faced lots of sexist comments when entering the world of sports presenting, however she has since become a respected BBC sports presenter that is only on the rise. Clare Balding has worked with ITV, BT and BBC as a female sports presenter and has become a face of female sports reporting. Balding is an award-winning sports presenter, former jockey with an OBE who has heavily worked in the world of football media.

Women in Business

Accountancy

There are 73,000 full-time chartered accountants and women make up 32,000 of these. In the UK, 44% of full-time accountants are female. In some cases, there are more women applying to accountancy firms than there are men for the first time in the firm’s histories. There is still a way to go with closing the gender gap in this line of work, however. While nearly half of people joining this profession are female, less than 25% of them are partners. This means there is still a need for ambitious and hard-working women to break the glass ceiling and gain those more senior roles.

Insurance and Acturial

It has been 97 years since the first woman actuary qualified in the UK. In 2013 women made up just 23% of qualified actuaries but there has been an increasing proportion of women in the profession. With a larger number of women in the profession nowadays it is unlikely a woman would feel uncomfortable and alone in a male dominated environment. Women made up 40% of new entrants to the profession meaning this profession is increasingly encouraging to young women. As with the rest of the corporate world it is difficult to see women at more senior levels. This just emphasises the need for more determined and enthusiastic women in this sector to bring the equality to the more senior roles that is required.

Women in STEM

Technology

Astonishingly 5% of females hold leadership positions in technology, 78% of students cannot name a single famous woman working in technology, and 3% of females say a career in technology is their first choice. This is where we need to have change. With such strong role models for women in the technology industry we must strive to bring equality to the sector. Annie Easley, a NASA rocket scientist and an advocate for gender and racial diversity in STEM. Easley’s vital work on the Centaur rocket project has laid foundations for space shuttle launches of the future. Radia Perlman: The Mother of the Internet, invented the algorithm behind the Spanning Tree Protocol (STP) which was significant in making the internet today possible.

Science

Science is becoming more and more inclusive and many women are found to pursue futures in this area of STEM than any other. Who can blame them with such role models as Katherine Johnson, the American mathematician whose calculations of orbital mechanics as a NASA employee were critical to the sucess of the first and subsequent U.S crewered spaceflights. You might be fimiliar with her works in the movie "Hidden Figures". Another great female mind in the world of science is Dorothy Hodgkin, best known for her work in developing crystallography of biochemical compounds. Hodgkin’s won a Nobel Prize for her work with vitamin B-12.

Engineering

In 2017 21.8% of undergraduate engineering students were women. This figure is slowly on the rise but we are still no where near gender equality in this line of work. That is where we need the young women of today to be inspired. Beatrix Shilling was a British mechanical engineer who also served in the Royal Aircraft Establishment in World War II. Shilling developed a way for Spitfire planes to dive while in combat as well as holding the title of fastes female racer! There are also such incredible minds as Edith Clarke, who is known as the first female engineer. Clarke found it hard to earn a job as an engineer due to being female. After starting out studying civil engineering she dropped out of that course and pursued a degree in electronic engineering instead. After graduating, Clarke invented the Clarke Calculator (a mathematical technique that calculates long distance transmission line). More women are needed to make their mark on society and engineering is just the way to do it!

Women in the Arts and Literature

Literature

Every day, we get to honor the women out there who have done some extraordinary things, and this includes women in literature. Many writers throughout time have displayed their talents in a way that have changed the world of literature forever. We all want to believe that people out there understand us. We want to know that we can do great things and that we can inspire others as well. It becomes a chain of inspiration among writers, readers, and everyone else within their lives. When women share their voices, powerful things can happen. We have countless role models in the literature sector, specifically Jane Austen, the Brontë Sisters, Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, and Alice Walker, all of whom have made astonishing impacts in the world of literature.

Arts

Although women artists have been involved in the making of art throughout history, their work, when compared to that of their male counterparts, has been often obfuscated, overlooked and undervalued. However, Women in the British art world are in a better position than ever before. They’re increasingly holding top roles in the sector and arts organisations are working towards a better gender balance. Frances Morris was recently made Tate Modern’s first female director, and their galleries devoted three major 2015 shows to female artists – while at the Royal Academy of Arts, eight out of ten Royal Academicians elected last year were women, from the architect Farshid Moussavi to the painter Vanessa Jackson. They admit equal numbers of male and female students – and in fact, art schools across the country are commonly now populated by more female students than male.
